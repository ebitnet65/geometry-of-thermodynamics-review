\textbf{Overview of the intended review:}
Geometry has emerged repeatedly throughout thermodynamics, statistical mechanics, and quantum theory, appearing in contexts ranging from equilibrium fluctuation theory and critical phenomena to stochastic thermodynamics, information geometry, synchronization, and open quantum systems. These developments are often treated as independent applications of geometric methods. In this review, we argue that they instead reflect a common underlying principle: geometry is not a property of thermodynamic states alone, but of how those states respond to perturbations, constraints, environmental interactions, and observation.

We develop the concept of \emph{response geometry}, in which thermodynamic response is characterized by complementary symmetric and antisymmetric structures. The symmetric sector is associated with fluctuations, susceptibilities, stability, and statistical distinguishability, giving rise to thermodynamic, information-theoretic, and quantum metrics. The antisymmetric sector is associated with circulation, transport, irreversibility, and cyclic work, leading naturally to connections, curvature, geometric phases, and holonomy. From this perspective, fluctuations and work, information and transport, and equilibrium and nonequilibrium response emerge as different manifestations of a unified geometric framework.

Beginning with the historical development of thermodynamics from heat engines and equilibrium state spaces, we examine the geometric foundations of response in classical thermodynamics, critical phenomena, and stochastic dynamics. We then discuss synchronization and collective behavior as examples of emergent response manifolds generated through interactions and dissipation. Connections to information geometry, thermodynamic length, and quantum Fisher metrics are reviewed, followed by recent developments in open quantum systems, nonequilibrium steady states, and geometric descriptions of work and transport. Finally, we consider observable-dependent thermodynamics and observational geometry, where geometric structure depends explicitly upon the relationship between dynamics and measurement.

By synthesizing these diverse developments, we propose that response geometry provides a common language for thermodynamics across scales, linking the fluctuation theory of equilibrium systems to the nonequilibrium dynamics of complex classical and quantum matter. This viewpoint suggests new connections among thermodynamics, information theory, synchronization, and quantum technologies, while pointing toward a unified geometric theory of physical response.

“Geometry is not a property of thermodynamic states alone, but of how those states respond”

The examples surveyed in this review suggest that response geometry is not an auxiliary mathematical description but an organizing principle that transcends the microscopic details of individual physical systems. Equilibrium thermodynamics, statistical mechanics, open quantum systems, and nonlinear spectroscopy all admit formulations in terms of a common geometric language. From this perspective, geometry is not imposed upon thermodynamics; it is an intrinsic consequence of the structure of physical response.
